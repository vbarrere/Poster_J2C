\block{\strut 1. Scientific context, motivation, and approach}{
        
        \vspace{0.5cm}

        Electron microscopy makes it possible to study structures far smaller than the wavelength of visible light.
        In nanoparticle research, \textbf{high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM)} provides direct access to the \textbf{structure and morphology of nanoalloys}.
        However, interpreting HRTEM images remains \textbf{challenging, time-consuming, and highly expert-dependent}.
        \textbf{Deep learning} offers a promising route to accelerate and simplify this analysis while extracting information on the \textbf{structure and properties} of nanoalloys.
        Training such models, however, requires a \textbf{large and representative dataset}.
        Here, we generate this dataset from snapshots extracted from \textbf{molecular dynamics} simulations and from synthetic HRTEM images produced using the \textbf{multislice} method.
        
        \vspace{1cm}
        
        We focus here on the \textcolor{colorAg}{\textbf{Ag}}--\textcolor{colorCo}{\textbf{Co}} system, a well-known \textbf{immiscible nanoalloy}.
        In these particles, \textcolor{colorAg}{\textbf{Ag}} atoms preferentially segregate to the \textbf{surface}, whereas \textcolor{colorCo}{\textbf{Co}} atoms tend to occupy the \textbf{core}.
        This behavior is expected because \textcolor{colorAg}{\textbf{Ag}} has a lower surface energy than \textcolor{colorCo}{\textbf{Co}}, and \textcolor{colorAg}{\textbf{Ag}} atoms are also larger.
        Depending on the synthesis conditions, however, these nanoalloys may adopt \textbf{metastable conformations}, ranging from \textbf{core--shell} and \textbf{Janus} structures to \textbf{partially mixed} configurations.
        This system therefore provides an ideal benchmark to test whether \textbf{deep learning} can reliably extract structural information from HRTEM images.
