\block{Introduction}{
        
        \vspace{0.5cm}

        Electron microscopy allows us to study the structure of objects much smaller than the wavelength of visible light.
        In the field of nanoparticles, high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) enables us to investigate the structure and geometry of nanoalloys.
        However, analyzing HRTEM ima!ges can be time-consuming and challenging.
        It needs a lot of expertise and experience in the imaging field to analyze them correctly.
        The use of deep learning can be an option to accelerate and simplify the analysis of these HRTEM images and exctract information about the structure and properties of nanoalloys.
        But for this, a dataset is needed to train the deep learning models.
        This dataset is generated using a molecular dynamics code developed in the laboratory.
        
        \vspace{0.5cm}
        
        To start this PhD project, we focus on the \textcolor{colorAg}{\textbf{Ag}}–\textcolor{colorCo}{\textbf{Co}} system, an immiscible alloy where the structure is known.
        In this system, \textcolor{colorAg}{\textbf{Ag}} atoms preferentially segregate to the surface while \textcolor{colorCo}{\textbf{Co}} atoms tend to form the core, consistent with their lower and higher surface energies, respectively.
        The objective with this system is to check if the deep learning models can learn and extract the same information from the HRTEM images.
        Once this objective is done, other systems with more complex structures that we don't know yet will be studied to extract new information about their structure and properties.



}
